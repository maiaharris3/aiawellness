Gut Health Is the Foundation of Everything. Here's Where to Start.

8 min read

Your gut contains trillions of microorganisms. They influence your mood, your immune system, your skin, your hormones, your cravings, your sleep, and almost every aspect of how you feel on a daily basis. This isn't alternative medicine anymore — it's mainstream research, and the more we learn, the more obvious it becomes: your gut is running the show.

The wellness internet has turned "gut health" into a $50 billion category, full of probiotic lattes, prebiotic sodas, $80 supplements, and a lot of people who want you to poop more. Some of it is real science. A lot of it is expensive guessing.

Here's what actually matters, in order of priority.

Why your gut runs so much

Your gut microbiome — the ecosystem of bacteria, fungi, and other microbes in your digestive tract — does way more than digest food. It:

  • Produces around 90% of your body's serotonin
  • Synthesizes essential nutrients, including vitamin K and several B vitamins
  • Regulates immune function (about 70% of your immune system lives in the gut)
  • Influences estrogen metabolism via a subset of microbes called the "estrobolome"
  • Produces short-chain fatty acids that reduce inflammation and support brain function
  • Communicates directly with your brain through the gut-brain axis

When your gut microbiome is diverse and well-fed, this whole system runs smoothly. When it's compromised — from antibiotics, chronic stress, a low-fiber ultra-processed diet, alcohol, or lack of sleep — everything downstream gets disrupted. That's why gut issues often show up as skin problems, mood dysregulation, immune dysfunction, hormonal weirdness, and autoimmune conditions. It's all one system.

Signs your gut is struggling

Some of these are obvious. Others aren't:

  • Bloating after most meals, not just specific ones
  • Gas that's excessive or unusually smelly
  • Constipation (fewer than one bowel movement a day, or consistently hard stools)
  • Diarrhea or alternating between the two
  • Acid reflux or heartburn
  • Acne, eczema, or unexplained skin rashes
  • Food sensitivities that seem to be multiplying
  • Brain fog, especially after eating
  • Frequent illness
  • Persistent fatigue
  • Intense sugar cravings
  • Low mood or anxiety that doesn't track with your actual life circumstances

You don't need all of these to have a gut issue. One or two persistent ones is enough to pay attention.

Where to actually start

In order of impact, from free to fancy.

1. Eat more fiber

This is the boring answer and the most important one. Most Americans eat 10–15g of fiber a day. The recommendation is 25–30g. Fiber is what feeds the beneficial bacteria in your gut. Without it, they starve and die off, and less helpful microbes take over.

Specifically, you want a variety of fiber sources. The Tim Spector-backed research out of King's College London showed that people who eat 30+ different plant foods per week have significantly more diverse microbiomes than people who eat fewer. Each plant feeds different microbes.

Easy ways to hit it: berries, leafy greens, beans, lentils, oats, nuts, seeds, sweet potatoes, artichokes, onions, garlic, apples. Aim for plants at every meal.

2. Add fermented foods

Fermented foods deliver live microbes and compounds that support gut function. A 2021 Stanford study found that a 10-week high-fermented-food diet increased microbiome diversity and decreased inflammatory markers more than a high-fiber diet alone. Both together is the best combination.

Real fermented foods: sauerkraut (refrigerated, not shelf-stable), kimchi, kefir, yogurt with live cultures, miso, tempeh, real sourdough bread, kombucha. Shelf-stable versions usually don't contain live cultures — check the label.

3. Reduce the stuff that wrecks it

You can eat all the kimchi in the world, but if you're also eating a diet that actively damages your gut, it won't matter. The biggest disruptors:

  • Ultra-processed food. Emulsifiers (polysorbate 80, carboxymethylcellulose), artificial sweeteners (sucralose, aspartame), and industrial additives have been shown in human and animal studies to negatively affect the microbiome.
  • Alcohol. Even moderate drinking disrupts the gut lining and reduces microbial diversity.
  • Unnecessary antibiotics. Sometimes you need them. But your doctor should only be prescribing them when there's clear bacterial infection. Antibiotics can reduce microbial diversity for months to years after a single course.
  • Chronic stress. The gut-brain connection runs both ways. Stress changes your microbiome quickly.
  • Not sleeping enough. Microbiome composition is directly linked to sleep.

4. Hydration

Water matters for gut function more than people realize. Constipation is often just dehydration. Aim for steady intake throughout the day.

5. Think about probiotics — but carefully

Here's where the industry gets opportunistic. Most probiotic supplements are under-researched, and the strains in them may not survive your stomach acid, let alone colonize your gut. A random $60 probiotic from CVS is probably doing very little.

Probiotics with decent evidence for specific things:

  • VSL#3 / Visbiome — studied for IBS, ulcerative colitis
  • Saccharomyces boulardii — studied for preventing antibiotic-associated diarrhea
  • Lactobacillus rhamnosus GG — studied for various conditions, one of the most-researched strains

For most people without a specific condition, food-based sources of probiotics work better than a pill.

6. Prebiotics

Prebiotics are the food that feeds probiotic bacteria — basically specific types of fiber. You get them from garlic, onions, asparagus, leeks, Jerusalem artichokes, oats, bananas, and apples. You don't need a supplement. You need to eat plants.

What to skip

  • Colon cleanses. They don't detox you. Your liver does that. Cleanses can actually damage the gut lining and strip out beneficial bacteria.
  • Most "gut health" drinks at Erewhon. Nice to have, won't change your baseline.
  • Expensive at-home microbiome tests. The science on interpreting them is still too immature to act on. The report you get is interesting, not actionable.
  • Restrictive "elimination diets" without guidance. Eliminating whole food groups long-term can actually reduce microbial diversity, making things worse.

When to see a doctor

If you have persistent GI symptoms, see a gastroenterologist. Not a functional medicine practitioner who sells supplements on the side. Real symptoms deserve real workup — blood in stool, unexplained weight loss, persistent pain, major changes in bowel habits are all red flags that need medical attention, not kombucha.

The takeaway

Your gut health is 90% about what you put in your mouth every day. Fiber from 30+ plants a week, real fermented foods, minimal ultra-processed food, enough water, enough sleep, and stress management will do more for your gut than any supplement stack. Start there. Everything else — the probiotics, the glutamine, the bone broth, the expensive tests — is optional, and most of it is optional in the "probably useless" sense.

Gut health is not a product. It's a pattern.
