What "Clean" Beauty Actually Means (And What Brands Don't Want You to Know)

6 min read

"Clean" is the most abused word in beauty. A brand can slap it on a product that contains phenoxyethanol, PEGs, synthetic fragrance, and chemical sunscreen filters — and there's no legal consequence. That's not because the brands are breaking rules. It's because there are no rules.

Here's what "clean" actually means, what it doesn't, and how to read past the marketing.

The regulatory reality

"Clean" has no legal definition in the United States. None. The FDA does not define it. The FTC does not define it. A product labeled "clean beauty" has met exactly zero required standards to use that word.

The cosmetics industry in general is remarkably unregulated. The Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act has been in place since 1938, and until 2022, the FDA only had the authority to ban 11 specific ingredients from personal care products. The EU bans over 1,300. That's not a typo.

In 2022, Congress passed the Modernization of Cosmetics Regulation Act (MoCRA), which expanded FDA authority for the first time in decades. MoCRA now requires companies to register their facilities, submit ingredient lists, and maintain safety records. This is meaningful progress. But MoCRA still doesn't define "clean," doesn't regulate the term, and doesn't restrict most of the ingredients that sparked the clean beauty movement in the first place.

So when a brand markets itself as "clean," they're making a marketing claim, not a regulatory one. And the claim is whatever they say it is.

The words brands hide behind

Every brand's "clean" definition is different, and most are carefully worded to sound more rigorous than they are. Here are the most common terms and what they actually mean:

"Clean" — Legally meaningless. Varies wildly by brand.

"Natural" — Also legally meaningless. Cyanide is natural. Sun exposure is natural. This word tells you nothing.

"Non-toxic" — Unregulated term. Water is toxic in the right dose. Without context, this is nothing.

"Plant-based" — Can mean the product contains one plant-derived ingredient alongside 30 synthetic ones.

"Dermatologist-tested" — Means a dermatologist was involved at some point. Doesn't mean it's safe or clinically proven.

"Hypoallergenic" — Has no standardized definition. Brands set their own bar.

"Fragrance-free" — This one is meaningful. If a product is labeled fragrance-free, it should not contain synthetic fragrance. But "unscented" is different — an unscented product can contain masking fragrance. Read both words carefully.

"Free of parabens, sulfates, and phthalates" — Specific ingredient exclusions are meaningful, but only if you understand what else might be in the product.

Ingredients to actually watch for

Instead of relying on the word "clean," read the ingredient list. Here's a shortlist of things that show up in products marketed as clean but should raise questions. This isn't exhaustive — it's the ones that slip past most people:

Butyloctyl salicylate. In almost every "clean mineral sunscreen." Flagged by the EU for potential reproductive toxicity concerns. The single most common offender.

PEGs (polyethylene glycols). PEG-7, PEG-40, PEG-100, and so on. Petroleum-derived and can be contaminated with 1,4-dioxane, a likely human carcinogen, depending on manufacturing.

Phenoxyethanol. A preservative. EWG rates it moderate risk; restricted in Europe to below 1% and not allowed in products for children under 3.

"Natural flavor" and "parfum." Either can legally contain dozens of undisclosed compounds, including phthalates. If a product says "fragrance-free" and you see "parfum" anywhere, that's a contradiction.

Chemical UV filters. Oxybenzone, octinoxate, homosalate, octisalate, octocrylene, avobenzone. Not confirmed Generally Recognized as Safe and Effective (GRASE) by the FDA, and several are under active investigation for endocrine activity.

Formaldehyde releasers. DMDM hydantoin, quaternium-15, imidazolidinyl urea, diazolidinyl urea, bronopol. Slowly release formaldehyde as a preservative. Show up in some "natural" shampoos.

The "clean beauty" brands that fail their own standard

We've covered this in our sunscreen testing and it applies broadly. Brands that market themselves as clean but regularly fail full-ingredient screenings include:

  • Supergoop (chemical UV filters across most of their line)
  • Colorescience (butyloctyl salicylate, PEGs, phenoxyethanol)
  • Drunk Elephant (phenoxyethanol, various concerns depending on product)
  • Beautycounter (phenoxyethanol across most of their line; also shut down in 2024)
  • Coola ("organic" sunscreens with full chemical filter stack)

This doesn't mean these brands are trying to deceive anyone. It means "clean" doesn't mean what most consumers think it means, and being a "clean" brand doesn't require passing any actual standard.

How to actually evaluate a product

Skip the marketing. Read the ingredient list. A few heuristics:

1. Short list = generally better. The fewer ingredients, the fewer opportunities for something problematic. This isn't a hard rule — some good products are complex — but it's a decent starting filter.

2. The first five matter most. Ingredients are listed in descending order by concentration (until you hit the under-1% group). If the first five are recognizable as things that should be in a product — zinc oxide, water, aloe, shea butter, vitamin E — that's usually a good sign.

3. Cross-reference with EWG Skin Deep. It's not perfect, but it's free and it's better than nothing. You can look up individual ingredients and see their hazard ratings based on available research.

4. Check Yuka if you want a score. The Yuka app scans products and gives a 1–100 rating based on their ingredient database. Not foolproof, but useful.

5. If you can't find the full ingredient list, don't buy it. This is non-negotiable. Brands that hide the full ingredient list are banking on you not looking.

Why this matters

Your skin is your largest organ. Products you apply to it daily — moisturizer, sunscreen, deodorant, hair products, makeup — compound over years. You're not going to be poisoned by your face cream, but chronic low-level exposure to endocrine-disrupting ingredients, over a lifetime, does add up. The precautionary principle here is reasonable: reduce exposure where you can, don't lose sleep over one product.

The deeper point is that the word "clean" has lost its meaning because every brand has a financial incentive to claim it. The only way to actually know what's in your products is to read the label yourself.

The takeaway

"Clean beauty" is a marketing category, not a regulatory one. The word tells you nothing about what's in a product. The only reliable way to evaluate a formula is to ignore the front of the package and read the ingredient list. Short lists are usually better. Specific claims ("paraben-free," "fragrance-free") are more useful than vague ones ("natural," "clean"). And when a brand makes it hard to find the full ingredient list, there's a reason.

Don't trust the word. Trust the label.
