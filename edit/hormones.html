Your Hormones Are Running the Show — Here's How to Work With Them

7 min read

The reason your energy tanks in the afternoon, your skin breaks out on a schedule, your workouts feel impossible one week and easy the next, and your cravings get weirdly specific — it's almost always hormones.

This isn't a woo-woo framing. Your endocrine system is a communication network that runs literally every major system in your body: metabolism, reproduction, stress response, sleep, mood, hunger, immunity, skin turnover. If your hormones are dysregulated, downstream everything gets weird.

The good news: you don't have to be a passenger. Here's how to work with your hormones instead of against them.

The big players

You don't need to memorize the whole endocrine system to function. But there are five hormones that dominate day-to-day experience, especially for women:

Estrogen. Rises in the first half of your cycle, peaks right before ovulation, drops after. When it's high, you feel sharper, more social, more energetic, skin glows. When it's dropping, PMS territory.

Progesterone. Rises after ovulation, dominates the second half of the cycle. Calming, body-temperature-raising, appetite-increasing. Low progesterone is a common driver of bad PMS, anxiety, and disrupted sleep.

Testosterone. Yes, women have it too. Supports libido, muscle mass, motivation, focus. Tanks with age, stress, and chronic dieting.

Cortisol. Your stress hormone. Follows a daily rhythm — high in the morning to wake you up, drops through the day, low at night. When chronic stress flattens this curve, everything else gets disrupted.

Insulin. The one that gets less airtime but is arguably the most important. Controls blood sugar, fat storage, hunger signals, and influences every other hormone on this list. Insulin resistance is the root of a lot of hormonal dysfunction people don't realize is hormonal.

The cycle actually matters

If you have a regular menstrual cycle, you're operating in a four-phase hormonal environment that repeats every month. Most workout plans, diet plans, and productivity systems are built for a steady-state body — which is a male body, biologically speaking. Women are not steady state. And trying to be produces a lot of frustration.

Menstrual phase (days 1–5). Hormones are at their lowest. Energy is low. Honor it. Rest more, eat warm foods, don't try to PR at the gym.

Follicular phase (days 6–14). Estrogen rises. You feel better. Energy climbs. This is when to schedule harder workouts, bigger social commitments, creative work.

Ovulatory phase (around day 14). Peak estrogen. You're at your most verbal, most social, most magnetic. Great time for anything high-stakes — interviews, dates, presentations.

Luteal phase (days 15–28). Progesterone rises, then drops. Energy dips. Appetite increases (which is normal — your body actually needs about 200 extra calories during this phase). Sleep can get weird. This is not the time for restrictive diets or crushing workouts.

Working with this instead of ignoring it is one of the highest-leverage things you can do.

What disrupts your hormones

Almost everything about modern life is hormonally disruptive. Not in a panic way — just in a "here's what's actually going on" way.

  • Chronic stress. Prolonged cortisol elevation suppresses reproductive hormones, disrupts sleep, and drives insulin resistance. This is the number one hormone disruptor.
  • Not sleeping enough. Seven hours is the floor. Fewer than six wrecks insulin sensitivity by something like 25% the next day.
  • Under-eating (especially carbs). Chronic caloric restriction and super-low-carb diets can suppress thyroid function and reproductive hormones. Bodies read "not enough food" as a threat and downregulate non-essential systems.
  • Endocrine disruptors in daily products. Phthalates, parabens, BPA, PFAS — these are chemicals that mimic or block hormones in the body. They're in conventional skincare, makeup, food packaging, receipts (yes, receipts), and water. Reducing exposure doesn't require perfection. It requires attention.
  • Over-training. Chronic high-intensity cardio without adequate recovery elevates cortisol and suppresses sex hormones.
  • Alcohol. Directly impacts estrogen metabolism and liver function. Even moderate drinking affects cycles.
  • Ultra-processed food. Drives insulin resistance, inflammation, and disrupts the gut microbiome — which plays a bigger role in hormone metabolism than most people realize.

What actually helps

Eat enough protein. At least 0.7–1g per pound of goal body weight. Protein stabilizes blood sugar, provides amino acids for hormone synthesis, and supports muscle, which is itself hormonally active tissue.

Eat enough carbs, strategically. Complete carb avoidance is a fast track to wrecked reproductive hormones. Whole-food carbs — potatoes, rice, fruit, oats, beans — support ovulation and thyroid function. The fear of carbs is misplaced; the fear of refined sugar is reasonable.

Strength train. Muscle is the most metabolically active and insulin-sensitive tissue you have. Two to four strength sessions a week does more for your hormonal picture than any supplement.

Protect sleep. Cool, dark room, consistent schedule, no alcohol before bed, limit screens in the last hour. Sleep is when your hormones rebalance.

Manage stress, actually. "Reduce stress" is useless advice. Specific practices that measurably lower cortisol: 10 minutes of walking outside, slow nasal breathing (box breathing works), time off social media, real human contact, therapy if you need it.

Reduce your exposure to endocrine disruptors. Swap conventional skincare and cleaning products for cleaner options. Drink filtered water. Avoid plastic food containers when possible. Decline the receipt. These aren't crunchy additions — they're small daily reductions in total load.

Get tested if something feels off. A full hormone panel (thyroid, sex hormones, cortisol, insulin, fasting glucose, HbA1c) is worth it. Symptoms like persistent fatigue, hair loss, irregular periods, bad PMS, unexplained weight changes, or low libido deserve real data, not guesses.

The takeaway

You don't need to biohack your hormones. You need to stop fighting them. Your body is running a sophisticated operating system, and most of what wellness culture sells you is trying to override it. The move is the opposite: figure out what your hormones are doing, give them what they need, and stop treating every cycle phase like the same day on a loop.
