Seed Oils: The Ingredient You're Eating Every Day Without Knowing

7 min read

Seed oils are the most argued-about food ingredient of the last five years. One side says they're poison and the reason for every chronic disease. The other side says they're health food and the fear is internet hysteria. The truth is messier than either camp wants to admit — and it's worth understanding, because seed oils are in almost everything you eat.

Let's get into what's actually going on.

What seed oils are

"Seed oils" refers to eight industrially produced oils extracted from — you guessed it — seeds: canola, corn, cottonseed, grapeseed, rice bran, safflower, soybean, and sunflower. Sometimes called the "hateful 8" by critics, they're the cheapest fats in the food system. Restaurants fry in them. Processed foods are built on them. "Vegetable oil" on a label is almost always one of these.

They're distinct from fruit oils (olive, avocado) and traditional fats (butter, ghee, lard, tallow, coconut oil), which come from different parts of plants or animals and have different fatty acid profiles.

What the science actually says

Here's where it gets uncomfortable for both sides.

Against the fear narrative: The scientific evidence from major reviews and meta-analyses does not support the idea that seed oils themselves are driving inflammation or chronic disease when consumed in normal amounts. Multiple systematic reviews — including a 2024 analysis of nearly 1,900 people published in Nutrients, work from Johns Hopkins researchers, and position statements from the American Heart Association — have found that linoleic acid, the main omega-6 fatty acid in seed oils, is associated with lower markers of inflammation and lower risk of cardiovascular disease. Replacing saturated fats with polyunsaturated fats from seed oils has consistent evidence for reducing heart disease risk.

The "seed oils cause inflammation" theory is based on a biochemical pathway — linoleic acid converts to arachidonic acid, which is a precursor to inflammatory molecules. But only about 0.2% of dietary linoleic acid actually converts. And arachidonic acid also produces anti-inflammatory signaling molecules. The body is more complicated than the TikTok version.

Against the "seed oils are health food" narrative: The issue isn't really the oil itself. It's the context.

  • Seed oils are the dominant fat in ultra-processed food. When you eat seed oils, you're almost always eating them alongside refined flour, added sugar, sodium, and industrial additives. The ultra-processed food is the actual health problem, not the oil in isolation.
  • Oxidation matters. Polyunsaturated fats are unstable and prone to oxidation, especially when repeatedly heated. The oil that's been sitting in a fast-food fryer for three days is not the same as fresh oil from a bottle. Oxidized fats are genuinely inflammatory, and restaurant frying is one of the worst applications.
  • The processing is aggressive. Seed oils are industrially refined — typically extracted with chemical solvents (hexane), then bleached, deodorized, and stabilized. Hexane residue is regulated and trace amounts are within safety limits, but this is not a mechanical press operation. It's industrial chemistry.
  • The ratio is imbalanced, even if that's not the main issue. Omega-6 to omega-3 ratios in modern Western diets are around 10:1 or higher. Scientists debate whether the ratio itself is a problem or whether people just need more omega-3. Either way, most people would benefit from more omega-3 (fatty fish, walnuts, flax) regardless of what's happening with seed oils.

So what's AÏA's position?

We screen out seed oils as a primary ingredient in food products because the products that contain them are almost always ultra-processed, and because the oxidation issue with restaurant frying is real. A brand that uses high-quality avocado oil or olive oil is making a different choice than a brand using refined soybean oil as a cost-saving vehicle. That choice signals something about the rest of the formulation.

That's different from saying seed oils are poison. They're not. If your parents cook with canola oil at home, that's fine. If you eat fast food fried in reused seed oil five times a week, that's a different conversation — and the issue there is more about the frying and the overall diet than the oil itself.

What to actually do

Practical advice, because abstract nutrition debates don't help anyone make lunch.

Cook with: extra virgin olive oil for most things, avocado oil when you need higher heat, butter and ghee for flavor, occasional coconut oil. These are stable, minimally processed, and bring something to the dish.

Reduce exposure to: restaurant deep-fried food (the fries, the chicken tenders, the chips), heavily processed snack foods where the first oil listed is canola/soybean/sunflower, salad dressings where the base is "vegetable oil" instead of olive.

Don't stress about: a homemade meal cooked in canola oil, store-bought mayo, or the occasional packaged food that uses sunflower oil. Total diet quality matters way more than hunting every molecule of linoleic acid out of your pantry.

Increase: omega-3 sources. Wild salmon, sardines, walnuts, chia, flax. This is the actual nutritional deficit for most people.

The big picture

The seed oil conversation has become a proxy for a bigger, more accurate observation: ultra-processed food is making us sick. Seed oils are in almost every ultra-processed food. So if you avoid seed oils, you probably also reduce your intake of refined grains, added sugar, and industrial additives — and you will feel better. But the feel-better is about the ultra-processed food, not the oil itself.

Meanwhile, the anti-seed-oil wellness movement has pushed some people toward tallow and lard as if those are magically superior. The saturated fat evidence is unchanged: replacing saturated fat with unsaturated fat lowers cardiovascular risk. That's one of the best-supported findings in nutrition science, going back decades.

The honest answer sits in the middle. Seed oils are not poison. Ultra-processed food built on seed oils is genuinely bad for you. Replacing your everyday cooking oil with olive and avocado is a good move — not because you're detoxing, but because you're eating less processed food and better-tasting fats. Both can be true.

The takeaway

Seed oils aren't the boogeyman. Restaurant-fried, repeatedly-heated seed oil in ultra-processed junk food is a real issue. Home cooking with olive and avocado oil is a solid default. Adding more omega-3s is probably more valuable than obsessing over seed oils. Don't let the wellness internet scare you into replacing your grandma's canola-fried eggs with beef tallow.
